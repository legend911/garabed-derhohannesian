Dorothy Adlow

Art Critic

Christian Science Monitor - May 1950

Garabed DerHohannesian is one of the most resourceful and sensitive artists in Boston. In long, studious researches with color he has learned to use it with meaning and grace. He works in pure abstraction…through sensitive juxtaposition, he produces sequences, patterns of movement, modulations of hue, super-positions, augmentations, condensations and rarefactions.

He fuses light color [and] line. He produces a dynamic on the pictorial surface which parallels musical composition. Sometimes he sets a line pattern across the figuration of veiled planes, thus producing the effect of a melodic line against an accompaniment of chords…[he] is resourceful with various media as well. He works in oil on gesso, gouache, watercolor, mat board, metallic paper and casein panel on gesso…[he] works with a gentle, modest hand. The non-objective pattern comes to be a language in itself, beautifully articulate of thought and feeling.

Art Critic

Unknown Publisher

As a painter, Garabed DerHohannesian brings to his own work a superb sense of design, structure and balance using color, line, light or form. Although he feels a firm connection to and inspiration from his environment, he expresses this by creatively exploring the design relationships of geometric shapes, color balance and seemingly meandering line.

His work ranges from pure abstraction using color or line in dynamic juxtaposition to suggestions of reality that, with geometric patterns, shaded tonalities and textures, gave an optical illusion of three-dimensional space. Or, with meticulous pen and ink drawing and wash, he would invent organic forms or suggest a hidden marsh landscape veiled in fog. There is a vibrancy and dynamic in his compositions carefully controlled by a formality of design skill but always a great enjoyment and zest in the complexity of shapes in tilting blocks of color, in complex line patterns, or webs and vistas of space created by his forms and line.

Bradford F. Swan

Art Critic

Providence, RI Journal - April 1952

Garabed DerHohannesian…has always been a very elegant artist. He works with infinite care and he produces works which have a real feeling of sensitivity, whether they are drawings of weed-filled pastures or Op-ish abstractions based on the building blocks of a city.

What impresses me most about his work is that he always seems to be his own man. He never works anyone else’s side of the street.

But that, of course, is not enough to account for the very handsome things he turns out…he proves he is equally at home working in all-white…or in brilliant color. The bravura technique of which he is a master can be readily seen.

Boston Herald

November 11, 1962

The same reverberations distinguish Garabed DerHohannesian’s latest solo of drawings at Carl Siembab’s [gallery] this month. They range from vibrant and bold abstracts in colored ink to fluent, lyrical landscape, chiefly marsh grasses drawn with a meticulous and observant draughtmanship.

Through diminutive cross-hatchings and swift, controlled strokes, Mr. DerHohannesian introduces engaging tonalities and textures into his designs. Some of the ink paintings are objects viewed in close-up… these are effectively patterned, while the color in the abstractions discloses subtlety and brilliance.

Nonetheless, the most distinguished element comprised the landscapes. The artist captures the rank, swampy character of the terrain, combined with the structural impact it makes en masse, the isolated wintry splendor of a dense bare climate. The gentleness with which he treats this attains poetic ease and in the drawing “Thaw” geometric elements lend a dimension of rare subjectivity as angled slabs recede toward a murky horizon.
